Welcome to Data Science at Olin College, Spring 2020.

Calendar

Instructor: Allen Downey

Meetings: Monday and Thursday, 1:30 to 3:10pm, AC417.

Textbook: Spiegelhalter, The Art of Statistics

Topics

This course is about using data to answer questions and guide decision-making under uncertainty.

The primary goal is to learn and practice an end-to-end data science process that includes:
  1. Formulating questions, finding or collecting data, and choosing methods.
  2. Understanding the context of the problem, the potential for positive impact, and the ethical issues around the use of data.
  3.  Bringing data into a computational environment, cleaning, validating, and exploring it.
  4. Using visualization and summary statistics to describe and quantify distributions and relationships among variables. 
  5. Choosing methods and visualizations that are appropriate to the data and the question.
  6. Understanding the quality of the data and the limitations it imposes on the reliability of the results.
  7. Communicating a data story in verbal and written form, including effective data visualization.
  8. Using the tools and processes of reproducible science to allow others to see and use the code and data.
Students in this class develop a toolkit for working with real datasets, performing exploratory data analysis, and generating effective visualizations.  This toolkit includes:
  1. Basic programming skills in Python.
  2. Python libraries for working with data, primarily Pandas.
  3. Python libraries for statistical analysis, including StatsModels.
  4. Python libraries for visualizing data, primarily Matplotlib.
  5. Python libraries for maching learning, primarily scikit-learn.
  6. Tools for reproducible science, including Google Colab and GitHub.
Students work on projects where they practice this process, develop these skills, and have opportunities to communicate results to external audiences.

Policies

Work in this course includes
  • Reading and other preparation.
  • In-class exercises.
  • Homeworks.
  • Quizzes.
  • Reports.

Homeworks

Homeworks include exercises from a series of Jupyter notebooks and other materials.  These are primarily intended to help you learn, so they are evaluated on a coarse scale.

Quizzes

We will have 6-8 in-class quizzes covering material from the readings and in-class exercises.    These are intended to evaluate whether you are keeping up with the class and understanding the content.  They also give me feedback on which topics might need more work.

Make-up quizzes

I understand that students occasionally have to miss a class.  For that reason, the first missed quiz is excused (that is, the missed quiz is dropped).  If you have to miss a second quiz, please contact me as early as possible so we can schedule a makeup quiz.  If you don't contact me before the quiz, I cannot offer a makeup quiz.  If you miss a third quiz, I cannot offer a makeup quiz.  In those cases, I use a default grade of 4 out of 10, to limit the impact of missed quizzes.  If a student does not miss any quizzes, they get an "attendance bonus": their lowest quiz score is dropped.

Project

We will work on two projects over the course of the semester.

The first is based on a dataset and set of tools I have identified; the second is more open-ended.

You can work alone or in teams of two.  You can keep the same team for both projects or mix it up.

The goal of each project is to use data to tell a story.  I will provide examples of the kind of analysis and presentation you should do.

We will publish your reports in the form of (1) a blog post that presents the primary results and (2) a Jupyter notebook with the details.  Some reports might appear as guest articles in my blog, Probably Overthinking It, or in other venues.


Grading
Your final grade will depend on a weighted sum of your scores for homeworks, quizzes, and reports.  I will deduct some credit for late homeworks and reports, and for failures of professionalism.

Participation and professionalism

As students, you share responsibility for creating and maintaining a classroom atmosphere that is conducive to everyone's learning and enjoyment.  I hope you will think about how your participation contributes to the learning environment.

Some things you can do to help:
  1. Come to class.  I will do my best to use class time effectively.
  2. Come to class on time!   Late arrivals are disruptive.
  3. Come to class prepared.  Make sure you have always at least skimmed the reading.
  4. Try not to fall behind.  If we are all working on the same stuff at the same time, everything works better.
  5. Take care of your brain.  Eat well, sleep well, get some exercise.
  6. Be professional.  If you have to miss a class, or need to submit work late, communicate with the instructor.
  7. Be respectful toward the instructor and your fellow students.
  8. Be generous with your ideas and your time.  Help each other.
  9. Be reflective.  Think about what's working and what's not, and take responsibility for making the class work for you.
  10. Be honest.
  11. Have fun!
