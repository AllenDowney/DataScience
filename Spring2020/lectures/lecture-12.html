Mini-project report 1 notes

10 means ready to publish without further revision.

8 is the modal grade, meaning "as expected, could be published with some revision".

6-7 indicates reports that contain errors or require more substantial revision.

You have lots of choices about what to emphasize.  I think the differences between groups, in terms of absolute level, are the most immediate and reliable.  The trends are less reliable, and inflection points least reliable.

Most important: you need a mental model of what the reader does not know in order to give them the information they need to understand what you did, why, and what the results mean.

For the figures: decode before you interpret.  What are the dots, what are the lines?

If something goes from 50% to 45%, that's a decrease of 5 percentage points.  You could also say it's a decrease of 10%, but I would avoid that because it's ambiguous.

Round off, and use words to signal approximation.  In this example, we have about 1 digit of precision.

I think the "imaginary we" can be ok, but for something informal like a blog post, "I" is better.  Or at least explain who "we" is.

Several people got the coding backwards on the first attempt, and 2 people turned in reports with the coding backwards.  So that's a good cautionary tale!

Why did we recode responses with higher values for positive responses and maybe in the middle?

Why did we reduce political affiliation to 3 groups?

Tricky to get the level of detail right, but Python, Pandas, DataFrame, pivot_table, etc. is probably too much about the implementation.

How much to talk about resampling?

1) Some people included three resamplings; mostly, I think it distracted from the message.

2) For a short report, you should use resampling to be confident about the message, but the reader might not need to know.

"Political Alignment Case Study" is our name for this assignment, not a reader-centric title or headline.

Avoid self-reference like "In this report".  The report is not about the report.

(Weirdly, journalists have recently adopted the habit of referring to their own "reporting")


Verb tense.  We have three competing mental time lines:

1) The progress of your project (First we did this, then we did that)

2) The progress of the reader through the report.  (We will tell you... We told you.)

3) The 50 years of the GSS survey.

We want to talk about (3).  The others get in the way.

Art of Stats Chapter 6

Ways to summarize binary classification:

1) Error matrix (confusion matrix): accuracy, false positive, false negative, sensitivity, specificity.

2) As we vary the threshold, ROC, and AUROC.

3) Probabilistic predictions: calibration, Brier score, other cost functions.

We will get back to this, but to preview my opinion: binary classification is almost always a bad idea.

Better to generate some kind of propensity, and use an appropriate cost function.

Even better to generate a probability, which 

1) has a natural interpretation (although people need practice), and 

2) can directly inform decision-making.

Speigelhalter summarizes concerns about algorithms that have been raised elsewhere, notably in Weapons of Math Destruction.

We'll come back to that, too.
