Permutation testing

Permutation is one way to simulate the null hypothesis, particularly when testing differences between groups.

If your groups are actually the same, the group labels were arbitrary, so we might as well shuffle them.

On the Reddit statistics forum, about 25% of the questions are some form of "what test should I use?"

And 100% of my responses are "There is only one test."

But then it takes some time and thought to:

1) Choose a test statistic that quantifies the effect, and

2) Formulate and simulate the null hypothesis.

In your future lives, there is a good chance you will need to run a hypothesis test.  I encourage you to do it by running random simulations of the null hypothesis.

1) It's simple.

2) You get the right answer.

3) It requires you to elucidate* your model of the scenario.

* Literally "bring into the light"

Central Limit Theorem

According to our friends at Wikipedia:

The central limit theorem (CLT) establishes that, in some situations, when independent random variables are added, their properly normalized sum tends toward a normal distribution (informally a bell curve) even if the original variables themselves are not normally distributed.

This theorem is useful for two reasons:

1. In the context of mathematical statistics, it provides a way to approximate the sampling distribution of many statistics, at least "in some situations".

2. It offers an explanation for the ubiquity of normal distributions in the natural and engineered world.  If you measure something that depends on the sum of many independent factors, the distribution of the measurements will often be approximately normal.

And, as a corollary, if you measure something that is the product of many independent factors, the distribution of measurements will often be approximately lognormal.

More about this next time.

Interpreting medical tests


Medical tests are good examples, but everything in Bite Size Bayes notebook 5 applies to other kinds of binary classification.  More about that next week.

Let's assume that we know, for a given test:
  • Sensitivity: If the patient has the condition, what's the probability of a correct positive test?
  • Specificity: If the patient does not have the condition, what's the probability of a correct negative test?
Now suppose you get a positive test.  What is the probability you have the condition?

In terms of a Bayesian update, we have two hypotheses:
  • condition: the patient has the condition
  • no condition: the patient does not have the condition
And we have a datum: the positive test.  So what do we need to do an update?

1) The prior probabilities of the hypotheses.
2) The likelihood of the data under each hypothesis.

One of the likelihoods is easy: P(positive | condition) is sensitivity.

The other likelihood is not much harder: P(positive | no condition) is (1 - sensitivity).

But what about those pesky priors?  For that, we need the prevalence of the condition in a relevant population.

In the Bayesville example, we consider Jimmy to be a randomly chosen resident of Bayesville, where the known prevalence is 1%.

But the choice of prevalance is not always so easy:

1) Choosing the reference class is generally hard.

2) In many real-world scenarios, we don't know prevalence for any reference classes.

However, the primary point of this example is that

P(condition | positive test) != P(positive test | condition)

In the Bayesville example, the posterior is 16%, which is substantially less than sensitivity (and specificity).

If you think there is a 95% chance Jimmy has the condition, you have committed the base rate fallacy.

In the exercises, you ran two other scenarios:

1) If the base rate is 10%, the posterior probability is 68%, substantially higher than 16%, but still less than 95%.

2) If we improve the test, so sensitivity and specificity are 99.5%, the posterior probability is 67%.  Again, better than the less accurate test, but still not 99.5%.
