Art of Statistics Chapter 8

Remember last time I told you probability is hard?

Question 2

Suppose you get tested for a medical condition and the result is positive; that is, the test indicates that you have the condition.

The doctor says that the sensitivity of the test is 90%; that is, people who have the condition test positive 90% of the time.

Also, the specificity of the test is 95%; that is, people who don't have the condition test negative 95% of the time.

What is the probability that you actually have the condition?
Suppose the prevalence is 10%.

And suppose there are 1000 people in the population.

100 have the condition, 90 of them test positive (because sensitivity is 90%)

900 don't have the condition, 45 of them test positive (because specificity is 95%)

Of all the people who test positive, 90 / (90+45) = 2/3 have the condition

Or here's the example from Art of Statistics (with sensitivity 90%, specificity 90%, and prevalence 1%)



We will come back to this example a little later.

For people who said it depends on the false-negative rate, that is also true.  But the false-negative rate is the complement of specificity, so it is specified in the statement of the problem.  We'll come back to this, too.


Question 1

Suppose I flip a fair coin twice and tell you (honestly) that at least one of the coins is heads.  What is the probability that both coins are heads?
Ouch.

If you toss two coins, there are 4 equally likely outcomes: HH, HT, TH, TT

If I tell you that at least one is heads, that eliminates TT.  

The remaining 3 outcomes are still equally likely, so their probability is now 1/3 each.

Therefore, the probability of HH is now 1/3.
