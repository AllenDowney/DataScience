Correlation, determination, and error

Suppose you read a paper that reports that the correlation between the IQ of identical twins raised separately is 0.9 (I just made that up for this example; don't take it seriously).

1) What is the coefficient of determination, R²?

R² = 1 - Var(resid) / Var(y)

2) If we know one twin's IQ and use it to guess the other twins IQ, what percentage reduction in RMSE do we expect, compared to guessing the mean?

% improvement in RMSE = 1 - Std(resid) / Std(y)

Conclusion:

1) If slope is what you care about, report slope.

2) If predictive accuracy is what you care about, report RMSE or some other measure of predictive accuracy.

Whatever the question was, correlation is not the answer.

Quiz 3 notes


Question 1: A few of you found Series.mode(), which find the most common value in a Series.

So it works for a Series like s = np.Series([1,2,2,3])

But if we compute the PMF of that Series, we get

1 0.25
2 0.5
3 0.25

And if we apply mode to that series, the result is 0.5.

But if we apply idxmax, we get the quantity with the highest probability, 0.5.

This is a hole in the empiricaldist API.  I will fix it.

Question 2: Also related to the empiricaldist API:  

If you know a quantity is in the CDF, you can use []

Otherwise, use () or forward(), which interpolate.

Question 3: Several people gave good answers to different questions.

central tendency or location: mean, median, mode, "average", "typical"

spread: standard deviation, IQR, range, variance, coefficient of variation, "variability"

skewness: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skewness#Sample_skewness

kurtosis: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurtosis#Sample_kurtosis

Skewness is visible in PMF or KDE, and a little harder to get from CDF.  Quantifying it is hard and seldom useful.

Quantifying kurtosis is even harder and less useful.

If you encounter someone spiraling into the skewness/kurtosis black hole, you can help them by asking "why" five times.

Question 4: 

select a column:  df['colname']

select a row: df.loc[label]

select an element: df.loc[label, colname]

Also: df['colname'][label]  and df.get_value(label, 'colname')

Good idea to choose one idiom and stick to it.  Using loc[] is preferred.

Art of Statistics Chapter 5


Regression to the mean, a major source of cognitive error:

1) Before and after studies, like the traffic light example

2) Medical intervention: people often seek treatment when their condition worsens

3) A million versions of the observation that children of successful parents are less successful

And variations on Shirtsleeves To Shirtsleeves In Three Generations

And you can connect the dots from Telemachus (son of Odysseus) to The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire to Fin de siècle declinism and degeneration theory

4) Versions of the Sports Illustrated Cover Jinx

5) And Kahneman's story:

'I had the most satisfying Eureka experience of my career while attempting to teach flight instructors that praise is more effective than punishment for promoting skill-learning. When I had finished my enthusiastic speech, one of the most seasoned instructors in the audience raised his hand and made his own short speech, which began by conceding that positive reinforcement might be good for the birds, but went on to deny that it was optimal for flight cadets. He said, "On many occasions I have praised flight cadets for clean execution of some aerobatic maneuver, and in general when they try it again, they do worse. On the other hand, I have often screamed at cadets for bad execution, and in general they do better the next time. So please don't tell us that reinforcement works and punishment does not, because the opposite is the case." This was a joyous moment, in which I understood an important truth about the world: because we tend to reward others when they do well and punish them when they do badly, and because there is regression to the mean, it is part of the human condition that we are statistically punished for rewarding others and rewarded for punishing them. I immediately arranged a demonstration in which each participant tossed two coins at a target behind his back, without any feedback. We measured the distances from the target and could see that those who had done best the first time had mostly deteriorated on their second try, and vice versa. But I knew that this demonstration would not undo the effects of lifelong exposure to a perverse contingency.'

Key warning sign: if you select cases based on performance or any kind of output, and then measure again.

Regression, the statistical modeling method, is only related to "regression to the mean" by an accident of history and terminology.

Fundamental ideas:

1) Assume a function that relates a "dependent" variable to "independent" variables.  (Also called "endogenous" and "exogenous", and independent are called "predictors" or "explanatory variables")

The left side depends on the type of the dependent variable.

Can you identify these forms of regression?





2) Choose parameters that have some property you like.

Linear regression, it's usually minimizing RMSE, which is computationally efficient (and closed form).

Other forms of regression, usually maximizing the likelihood of the data. Almost as efficient, but iterative.
