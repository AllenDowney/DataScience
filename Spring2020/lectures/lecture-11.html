Simple regression


Lesson 1: Regression is not commutative.

y ~ x and x ~ y are different models with different interpretations


The orange line suggests a relationship that doesn't matter much in practice.
The green line suggests a substantial effect.

However...

Lesson 2: Regression alone doesn't say much about causation.

In this example:

1. Vegetable consumption cause increased income.

2. Higher income might cause higher vegetable consumption.

3. Other factors might cause both.

Can you think of plausible mechanisms for each?

Let's draw some causal graphs.

How could we test these hypotheses?


Multiple regression


With multiple regression, we can tease apart the effect of multiple explanatory variables.

In the example:  realinc ~ educ + age

We can think of this two ways:

1. Modeling the relationship between income and education, controlling for age.

2. Modeling the relationship between income and age, controlling for education.

This is one way to rule out a possible confounding variable.

At least tentatively.

Model selection and interpretation

We've seen groupby in the mini-project.

Logistic regression


If the dependent variable is a binary variable, we can think it is as a probability or "propensity"

We could use linear regression, but the predictions would not be probabilities.

With logistic regression, predictions are always between 0 and 1, and we can treat them as probabilities (subject to some assumptions).

With this model:

Intercept      0.348774
C(sex)[T.2]    0.741584
age            0.000417
educ           0.034725

Who is more likely to say yes to this question:

1. Males or females?

2. Old or young?

3. More educated or less?

What is the probability of answering "yes" for a 50-year old male with 16 years of education?
